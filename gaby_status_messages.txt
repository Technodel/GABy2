# ── "Did you know?" facts — shown every 60s during long tasks ─────
[did_you_know]
The first computer "bug" was a real insect — a moth found trapped in a relay of the Harvard Mark II computer in 1947. Grace Hopper's team literally debugged the machine.
Python was named after Monty Python's Flying Circus, not the snake. Guido van Rossum was reading Monty Python scripts when he started writing it.
The first version of JavaScript was written in just 10 days by Brendan Eich in 1995. It was originally called Mocha, then LiveScript, then JavaScript.
Git was created by Linus Torvalds in just 10 days in April 2005, after a licensing dispute left the Linux kernel without a version control system.
The word "robot" comes from the Czech word "robota," meaning forced labor or drudgery. It was coined by playwright Karel Čapek in 1920.
The term "byte" was coined in 1956 to mean 8 bits, specifically so it wouldn't be accidentally abbreviated to "bit."
TypeScript was created by Anders Hejlsberg — the same person who designed Turbo Pascal, Delphi, and C#. The guy just keeps inventing languages.
There are over 700 programming languages in existence. About 200 are in active use. Most developers use maybe 3.
The Apollo 11 guidance computer had 4 KB of RAM and 72 KB of ROM. Your smartwatch has roughly 100,000x more memory.
The most expensive software bug ever: NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter crashed in 1999 because one team used metric units and another used imperial. Cost: $327 million.
The first computer mouse was built in 1964 by Doug Engelbart — and it was made of wood.
"Hello, World!" as a programming tradition started with Brian Kernighan's 1972 C tutorial, making it over 50 years old.
Linus Torvalds originally wanted to name Linux "Freax," but the FTP server admin renamed the folder to "Linux" without asking.
React was created by Jordan Walke at Facebook and first deployed on the News Feed in 2011, years before it was open-sourced.
The HTTP 418 "I'm a teapot" status code is a real RFC standard — an April Fools' joke from 1998 that the internet refused to let die.
Vim was released in 1991. Generations of developers have since desperately typed :q! at least once in their lives.
The first 1 GB hard drive was the IBM 3380 (1980). It weighed 550 pounds, was the size of a refrigerator, and cost $40,000 to lease.
Node.js was created by Ryan Dahl in 2009. He later created Deno specifically to fix things he regretted about Node.js.
SQL was originally called SEQUEL until a trademark dispute with an aircraft company forced the rename.
The average programmer writes 10–50 lines of production code per day — not thousands. Most of the day is reading, thinking, and debating variable names.
The Agile Manifesto was written by 17 developers at a ski resort in Utah in February 2001.
An estimated 800 billion lines of COBOL code run in production today — mostly in banking, insurance, and government systems nobody wants to touch.
The JavaScript quirk typeof null === 'object' has been a known bug since 1995 and will never be fixed because too much production code relies on it.
The first ARPANET message ever sent (1969) was supposed to be "LOGIN." The system crashed after transmitting just "LO."
Claude Shannon, the father of information theory, once built a machine whose only function was to reach out, flip its own off switch, and go back to sleep.
The Raspberry Pi was designed to teach kids programming and accidentally became a cornerstone of global DIY electronics.
WebAssembly was designed so the exact same .wasm file runs identically across Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge, and Node.js.
NASA's Voyager 1 software, still operational since 1977, receives patches over a signal that takes 22+ hours one way to arrive.
The open-source movement got its name at a 1998 strategy session, just after Netscape released the Navigator source code.
In 1984, Ken Thompson proved in his Turing Award lecture you can backdoor a C compiler so it survives even perfectly clean source code — the "Trusting Trust" attack.
There are more possible chess game variations than atoms in the observable universe — estimated at 10^120.
The space bar is the most-pressed key on a keyboard. The second most pressed? Backspace. Make of that what you will.
70% of software bugs are caused by misunderstood or incomplete requirements — not bad implementation.
PHP originally stood for "Personal Home Page" tools. It now officially stands for "PHP: Hypertext Preprocessor" — a recursive acronym.
The first iPhone didn't support copy and paste. Apple added it two full years after launch in iPhone OS 3.0.
DOOM (1993) has been ported to more platforms than any other software in history — including a pregnancy test, a Martin guitar, and an ATM.
The first URL ever, http://info.cern.ch, went live on August 6, 1991. It's still online today.
SHA-256, used in Bitcoin, is a one-way function — reversing it by brute force would take longer than the current age of the universe.
The original Macintosh team flew a pirate flag over their building at Apple. Steve Jobs wanted them to feel like rebels within the company.
Fewer than 1% of GitHub repositories contain 99% of all forks and stars on the platform.
Reading code uses a completely different part of the brain than reading natural language — it activates the multiple demand network instead of the language area.
The average web page grew from 14 KB in 1995 to over 2 MB today — a 140x increase driven mostly by JavaScript bundles.
The "10,000 hours to mastery" rule comes from a 1993 study — and the original paper says deliberate practice matters far more than just hours logged.
Stack Overflow was founded in 2008. By 2010, just 1,000 users had answered 85% of all questions ever posted on the site.
The word "debugging" predates computers — it was used in radio and electrical engineering long before Grace Hopper's famous moth.
The spinning beach ball on macOS is officially the "Spinning Wait Cursor" — or affectionately, SPOD: the Spinning Pinwheel of Death.
There are more IoT devices connected to the internet than people on Earth — by a factor of roughly 3 to 1.
The `git blame` command was almost named `git praise` in an early patch — then someone changed it before merging.
